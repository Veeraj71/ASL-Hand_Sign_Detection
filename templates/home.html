ASL Fingerspelling Alphabet

The American Sign Language alphabet, also called the ASL fingerspelling alphabet, is a set of 26 hand gestures used to spell out words. Fingerspelling is commonly used for proper names, technical terms, or words that don't have a specific sign.

About American Sign Language

What is ASL?

American Sign Language (ASL) is a complete, natural language that has the same linguistic properties as spoken languages, with grammar that differs from English. ASL is expressed by movements of the hands and face.

It is the primary language of many North Americans who are deaf and hard of hearing, and is used by many hearing people as well.

History of ASL

ASL has its roots in French Sign Language (LSF). It was brought to America in 1817 when Laurent Clerc, a Deaf teacher from France, was recruited by Thomas Hopkins Gallaudet to help establish the first school for deaf students in the United States.

This school, now known as the American School for the Deaf, was founded in Hartford, Connecticut. The blend of LSF with local sign languages already in use created what we now know as American Sign Language.

ASL as a Language

ASL is a language with its own unique rules of grammar and syntax. It is not a form of English, nor is it universal—different countries have their own sign languages, just as they have different spoken languages.

Like all languages, ASL is a living language that continues to evolve over time. New signs are developed and old ones may change as the needs of the community change.

Misconceptions

One common misconception is that ASL is simply a signed version of English. In reality, ASL has its own grammar, syntax, and idioms that are distinct from English.

Another misconception is that sign language is universal. In fact, there are hundreds of different sign languages around the world, just as there are hundreds of spoken languages.

How Deaf People Communicate

Communication within the Deaf community and between Deaf and hearing individuals can take many forms. Understanding these methods helps bridge communication gaps and fosters inclusion.

Sign Language

The primary method of communication for many Deaf individuals is sign language. In the United States, this is primarily American Sign Language (ASL), which has its own grammar, syntax, and vocabulary distinct from English.

Fingerspelling

Fingerspelling uses handshapes that correspond to the letters of the alphabet to spell out words. It's commonly used for proper names, places, brands, or terms that don't have specific signs.

Facial Expressions

Facial expressions are an integral part of ASL grammar and can change the meaning of signs. They can indicate questions, emphasis, emotions, and other grammatical elements.

Body Language

Body positioning, movement, and gestures all contribute to meaning in sign language. The direction of signs, speed, and intensity can modify their meaning.

Written Communication

Many Deaf individuals use written English for communication, though it's important to note that for many, English is a second language after ASL.

Interpreters

Sign language interpreters facilitate communication between Deaf and hearing individuals by translating spoken language into sign language and vice versa.

Technology

Text messaging, video calls, social media, and specialized apps have revolutionized how Deaf people communicate, making distance communication more accessible.

Lip Reading

Some Deaf individuals use lip reading, but it's important to know that even the best lip readers typically only catch 30-40% of what is said, as many sounds look similar on the lips.

Deaf-Blind Communication

Those who are both deaf and blind may use tactile signing, where they place their hands over the signer's hands to feel the shapes and movements.

Deaf Culture

Deaf culture is rich and diverse, with its own values, traditions, art forms, and social norms. It's centered around the use of sign language and the shared experience of being Deaf in a predominantly hearing world.

Key aspects of Deaf culture include:

  • A strong sense of community and belonging
  • Valuing visual ways of communicating
  • Deaf literature, poetry, theater, and art
  • Social gatherings and organizations specifically for Deaf individuals
  • Educational institutions that cater to Deaf students
  • A belief that being Deaf is a cultural identity rather than a disability

Understanding and respecting Deaf culture is an important part of effective communication and interaction with the Deaf community.
